This visualization focuses on the general aspects of a person's life such as level of education, the importance attribued to family, national identity, politics, religion and work, as well as the amount of children and the overall life satisfaction, this last one is used to colorize the lines representing each country. Countries with satisfied citizens have warmer colors and dissatisfied are colder. During the second wave, data from a small sample of countries has been collected. Unfortunately, many variables are incomplete, therefore the representation is 0 on the parallel coordinates.

Analysis

In the third wave, more consistent and rich data is available. One can quickly notice the general agreement from all the surveyed countries about the importance of the family. From this data, we can see the negative correlation between education and the number of children that many experts already pointed out. More educated people rely less on their offsprings to support them. Interesting enough, if we brush the parallel coordinates for life satisfaction, during the mid 90s, the lower tier corresponds to the eastern European countries.

The fourth wave no longer holds data from those countries from eastern Europe, it would have been interesting to follow their response towards life satisfaction. Instead, the survey turns its focus towards African and Middle East countries. If we move the axis of Family next to the Work axis, we can notice how these two values are remarkably high. This explains the logic that in order to provide for the family, a stable work situation and source of income are equally important. Fortunately, no country places work above family.

How to read the survey values:
Children: average from 0 to +6 children
Education: average from 0 (no education) to 8 (university)
Family, Nationalism, Politics, Religion and Work: average from 0 (not at all important) to 3 (very important)
Life Satisfaction: average from 1 (very dissatisfied) to 10 (very satisfied)
